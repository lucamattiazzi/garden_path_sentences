Garden-Path Sentences in Gemma 3

Mechanistic-interpretability pipeline (steps 01–13) run on the Gemma 3 base-model family: 1B, 4B, 12B and 27B. All models are pre-trained checkpoints (-pt), evaluated in the same harness used for the Qwen3 investigation.

Overview

A garden-path sentence (GPS) such as “The old man the boat” leads the reader to commit to a wrong parse (“man” as noun) that must be revised at a disambiguator (“the boat” forces “man” to be a verb). This pipeline asks two questions of each model: does it notice the garden path (a surprisal spike at the disambiguating word), and does it recover from it (does comprehension after the disambiguator stay intact)? Two metrics carry the whole report:

  • Δ-surprisal — surprisal of the critical (disambiguating) word in the GPS version minus the same word in an unambiguous paraphrase. Large positive Δ = the model is surprised, i.e. it perceived the garden path.
  • vc_delta — surprisal of a verb-compatible continuation after the disambiguator, GPS minus normal. This is the comprehension cost: how much residual damage the garden path leaves after the model has had a chance to reanalyse.

Ablation steps measure how these metrics move when attention heads or whole layers are knocked out; patching and calibration steps (11–13) test whether any localisation claim survives scrutiny.

01 · Baseline garden-path effect

Mean Δ-surprisal at the critical word, with no intervention, across 50 GPS/normal sentence pairs spanning five syntactic templates: RR (reduced relative, “The horse raced past the barn fell”), NV (noun/verb ambiguity, “The old man the boat”), NP_S/NP_Z (subordinate-clause object/subject ambiguities) and COORD (coordination ambiguities). A positive bar means the model is more surprised by the critical word in the garden-path version — it took the wrong parse and noticed.

The per-pair distribution below shows the effect is heterogeneous: some pairs produce large spikes, others none (or even negative deltas, where the “normal” paraphrase happens to be the more awkward string).

02 · Layer sweep — where does the surprise signal live?

Each attention layer is zero-ablated in turn (all its heads) and the Δ-surprisal effect is re-measured. effect_drop = baseline Δ − Δ under ablation: positive means the ablated layer was contributing to the surprise signal. The dashed traces show control inflation — how much the same ablation inflates surprisal on simple unambiguous control sentences. A layer is interesting only if its effect_drop is large and its control inflation is small (i.e. it is GPS-specific, not just generally load-bearing). The x-axis is normalised depth (layer / n_layers) so the three sizes overlay.

03 · Head sweeps within candidate layers

For the layers flagged by step 02, each individual head is zero-ablated. Same reading as above: bars are effect_drop (Δ-surprisal lost when the head is removed). This is the step that would reveal a compact “garden-path circuit” — a few heads carrying most of the effect — if one existed.

04 · Do the surprise heads matter for comprehension?

The top candidate heads from steps 02–03 are ablated (individually, then all together) while measuring vc_delta — the post-disambiguator comprehension cost — instead of Δ-surprisal. If these heads implemented reanalysis, removing them should make comprehension worse (higher vc_delta than baseline). In the Qwen study they did not: Δ-surprisal-guided search finds prior heads, not comprehension heads. Bars are compared against the unablated baseline (first bar).

05 · Layer sweep on the comprehension metric

The same per-layer ablation sweep as step 02, but scored with vc_delta inflation: how much ablating the layer worsens the comprehension gap between GPS and normal continuations. Solid traces = vc_delta inflation; dashed = control inflation on unambiguous sentences. This asks where reanalysis-relevant computation lives, rather than where the surprise signal lives.

06 · Head sweeps on the comprehension metric

The direct hunt for a “reanalysis head”, with no detour through Δ-surprisal: inside the layers flagged by step 05, each head is zero-ablated individually while measuring vc_gps inflation — how much the ablation worsens the model's prediction of the verb-compatible continuation. A genuine reanalysis head should inflate vc_gps strongly and GPS-specifically (vc_delta up, controls flat). The dashed line, when present, is the selection-corrected null threshold from step 14: the 95th percentile of the best of K random heads, where K is the number of heads swept here — the fair bar for a “this head is special” claim. Note the scale against step 05: the whole-layer effect is not the sum of its heads — comprehension damage only appears when the layer's heads are removed together.

07/08 · Lexical floor — canonical vs novel NV pairs

A diagnostic for memorisation. For each NV pair, the verb-compatible continuation is scored after three prefixes: the full garden-path prefix (“The old man the”), a stripped prefix keeping only the ambiguous bigram (“man the”), and a neutral anchor prefix. Step 07 uses canonical (famous, plausibly memorised) pairs; step 08 uses novel pairs constructed to be absent from training data. If a model only “solved” canonical garden paths by n-gram memory, full should beat stripped on canonical pairs but not on novel ones. The error bar shows the bootstrap 95% CI on full − stripped. Note: this diagnostic was retired in the Qwen write-up — a flat full − stripped is compatible with both pure n-gram behaviour and successful reanalysis — so read it as descriptive, not as evidence.

09 · Comprehension cost with confidence intervals

The clean headline comparison: mean vc_delta (residual comprehension cost after the disambiguator) with bootstrap 95% CIs, for canonical vs novel NV pairs. A positive value means that even after seeing the disambiguator, the model is still worse at predicting a verb-compatible continuation in the GPS version — reanalysis is incomplete. Comparing across model sizes shows whether scale buys recovery.

10 · Attention patterns — does anything look back at the ambiguous word?

Tests the “retrospective read” hypothesis: at the disambiguator position, does some head attend back to the ambiguous word more in the GPS version than in the normal version? The heatmaps show, per (layer, head), the mean attention difference (GPS − normal) from the disambiguator to the ambiguous word; red = reads back more under GPS. The tables list the heads with the largest |difference| and the Spearman correlation between their per-pair attention difference and per-pair vc_delta (does reading back predict better comprehension?).

11 · Activation patching (NORMAL → GPS)

The denoising interchange: run the GPS sentence, but at one layer overwrite the residual stream at selected positions with the activation from the normal (unambiguous) run, and measure how much of the comprehension gap is recovered. recovery = 1 means patching that layer/position fully restores normal-sentence comprehension. Three position sets are patched: the ambiguous word (v_amb), the critical/disambiguating word, and both. Where the recovery curve peaks tells you at which depth, and at which token position, the verb-vs-noun information is carried. X-axis is normalised depth.

12 · Zero vs mean ablation

Calibration for the ablation results: zero-ablating a head sends the model off-distribution, which can manufacture effects. Here each finding is replicated with mean ablation (replacing the head's output with its dataset-mean output). Effects that shrink drastically under mean ablation were off-distribution artifacts; effects that survive are (more) real. Paired bars: zero vs mean, for each candidate head/head-set and layer.

13 · Empirical null distribution

Zero-ablation of 60 random single heads, giving an empirical null for “how big an effect does an arbitrary head produce?”. Any candidate head must beat this distribution before it can be called special. Histograms show the null over effect_drop (Δ-surprisal metric); the vertical lines mark the 5th/95th percentiles and the absolute maximum. In the Qwen 14B study no head exceeded the null — the test that dissolved the circuit claim.

14 · Empirical null for the comprehension metric

The calibration behind step 06. Many random single heads are zero-ablated while measuring vc_gps inflation (the cheap comprehension-only probe, so the sample count is much higher than step 13's). The histogram is the single-head null; because step 06 reports a selected maximum over the K heads it sweeps, the fair threshold is the bootstrap distribution of max-over-K draws from this null — marked as the dashed “max-of-K p95” line. The coloured line is the best head step 06 actually found: only if it clears the dashed line does the model have a comprehension head that beats selection-corrected noise.
